Cuba — Highs and Lows

Travel notes, 2000
Written in 2000 following travel in Cuba.
Names have been changed.

November 22nd, 2000. Cancún, Mexico.

I’m sitting comfortably on an Aero Cubana flight, headed for Havana, when a flight attendant leans over, hands out candy, and asks if this is my first time in Cuba.

“Yes,” I tell her — in terrible Spanish. Primera vez.

Then, in English: “I’ve been dying to go.”

An engine explodes behind my wing.

The pilot comes on the intercom, calm, routine. Minor technical issues.

A tourist in front of me turns around and says, dry as hell:

“Welcome to Cuba.”

Arrival

When we finally land in Havana, customs takes two. Long. Frustrating.

Instead of rushing outside, I collapse onto a bench and close my eyes.

Exhausted. Impatient. A little disillusioned.

Havana

Havana is intoxicating.

But if you step off the tourist routes — really step off — another Havana appears.

The poverty isn’t hidden.

But there is also sharing. A rough kind of equity.

Healthcare and education are universal. And good.

Revolution

Castro is, undeniably, a dictator.

Human rights violations are real.

Cuba is not simple.

But one thing is undeniable: the endurance of its people.

Pico Turquino

I see that endurance most clearly on Pico Turquino — the highest mountain in Cuba.

I turn back before the summit.

Angry. Exhausted. Furious at the guide. At them. At myself.

Later, two young Cuban women knock on my door.

One holds out a bowl.

“Ice cream, Michel?”

Closing

I experienced many highs and lows in Cuba.

But what stayed with me wasn’t ideology.

It was warmth. Resilience. Human dignity.

Viva Cuba.
